# From Syntax To Intent

For most of computing history, programming was an act of translation: understand
a problem in human terms, design a solution in the abstract, then render it in
syntax a machine can execute. As of the May 2026 paper *The New SDLC With Vibe
Coding*, that translation friction is collapsing. The paper frames the central
transformation as engineers moving "from writing code to expressing intent, and
trusting intelligent systems to translate that intent into working software."
In the new paradigm, developers state *what* they want built rather than *how*
to build it, and the machine handles the implementation.

This is not a fringe workflow. The paper reports that, as of early 2026, 85% of
professional developers regularly use AI coding agents, 51% use them daily, and
an estimated 41% of all new code is AI-generated. The center of gravity of the
job has already moved.

## What the machine takes over

The mechanical middle of programming — turning a settled design into working
syntax across many files — is the part agents now absorb. When intent is stated
clearly, producing the code that satisfies it is increasingly the machine's
work, not the engineer's keystrokes.

## What the engineer still owns

Handing off syntax does not hand off the job. The scarce human inputs become
*intent, architecture, and judgment*: deciding what to build, choosing the
structure it should take, and confirming that what came back is actually correct.
Expressing intent precisely enough that an agent can act on it is itself a skill,
and verifying the result is where human attention now concentrates rather than
in typing the solution by hand.

The shift, then, is less an elimination of engineering work than a relocation of
it — up the stack, from syntax toward intent and verification. The other
foundations concepts trace where that relocated work lands: how much structure
to wrap around AI output, and how the developer's role reshapes around directing
and reviewing rather than typing.
